Winter Letters · No. 8

The Warmth of a Known Routine

"Commit your works to the Lord, and your plans will be established."

— Proverbs 16:3

There is a version of repetition that feels like failure.

The same day again.

The same tasks, the same order.

The same cup in the same hand.

And then there is the other kind.

The kind that feels less like a rut and more like a foundation. The kind where the sameness is not a sign that nothing is happening, but a sign that something is being held.

A known routine is a form of care.

Not just for the people and animals who depend on it — the dogs who wait by the door, the husband whose lunch appears on the counter without being asked for — but for yourself.

There is something in the body that settles when the morning goes the way mornings go.

The coffee made the same way.

The familiar path through the kitchen.

The sequence of small tasks unfolding in their quiet order.

The hands already know what to do, and because of that the mind is free to be elsewhere.

This is not monotony.

Monotony is emptiness repeating.

A known routine is something different. It is a life that has slowly been shaped into something that works. Something steady. Something reliable.

The repetition is not the problem.

It is the point.

I have heard people say they want more spontaneity. More variety. More of the unexpected.

And I understand that.

But I have come to believe that spontaneity means more when it arrives inside a structure. That the unexpected guest is more welcome in a house that is already in order. That a day that breaks from routine is only a gift if you have a routine to break from.

The same mug.

The same chair.

The same window with the same light falling through it each morning.

These are not small things.

They are the quiet architecture of a life.

The things you have chosen — consciously or not — to return to each day because they work.

Because they hold the day steady before it has the chance to ask anything of you.

These are not small things.


They are the quiet architecture of a life.


That is the warmth of a known routine.
